# How to Spot a Phishing Scam in 2026

Searches for **"what is a phishing scam"** and **"what is a data breach"** are climbing — and for good reason. AI has made fake emails cleaner, fake voices realistic, and fake login pages pixel-perfect. The good news: almost every phishing attempt still trips at least one of the same seven wires.

## What phishing actually is

**Phishing** is any message — email, text (smishing), call (vishing), or DM — that impersonates someone you trust to get you to (a) hand over a password or payment, (b) click a malicious link, or (c) open an attachment. The goal is almost always your **credentials or money**.

## The 7 red flags (the checklist)

1. **Urgency or fear.** "Your account will be closed in 24 hours." Real companies rarely threaten.
2. **A link that doesn't match.** Hover (don't click). Does `paypa1-secure.com` really belong to PayPal? No.
3. **Generic greeting.** "Dear Customer" instead of your name.
4. **Unexpected attachment.** Invoices, "voicemails," shipping docs you didn't expect.
5. **Requests for credentials.** No legitimate company emails you asking for your password or full card number.
6. **Slightly-off sender address.** `support@micros0ft-help.com`. Look at the domain after the `@`, character by character.
7. **Too good to be true.** Refunds, prizes, crypto doubling. It isn't true.

## A real example, annotated

> **From:** Apple Support <no-reply@apple-id-verify.co>
> **Subject:** ⚠️ Your Apple ID has been locked
> *"We detected unusual activity. Verify within 12 hours or your account will be permanently disabled. [Verify Now]"*

Red flags: lookalike domain (`apple-id-verify.co`), urgency + threat, fear-driven CTA, generic. **Three wires tripped — delete.**

## What is a data breach (and why phishing causes them)

A **data breach** is when private data — emails, passwords, card numbers — is exposed or stolen. The most common entry point isn't a Hollywood hack; it's **one employee clicking one phishing link**. That's why awareness training beats almost any software.

## If you already clicked

1. **Don't panic, do move fast.**
2. **Change the password** for that account — and anywhere you reused it.
3. **Turn on two-factor authentication** (2FA) everywhere.
4. **Watch your accounts** for unfamiliar activity; tell your bank if money's involved.
5. **Report it** — to your IT team at work, or to the impersonated company.
