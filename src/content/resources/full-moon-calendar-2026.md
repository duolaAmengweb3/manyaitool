Searches for **"full moon tonight"** and **"blue moon"** spike every single month — and 2026 has a rare treat: **two blue moons logic** thanks to how the lunar cycle (~29.5 days) lines up with the calendar. ## Why a printed date isn't enough

A full moon happens at a single instant worldwide, but **moonrise** — when you actually see it climb over the horizon — shifts by your latitude and longitude. A calendar that says "June 29" can be off by a full evening depending on your city. That's why the smart move is to pair the dates below with a calculator that uses your location.

## How to actually see it

1. **Check moonrise, not the "full" instant.** The moon looks fullest the evening it rises near sunset.
2. **Find the horizon.** A low, unobstructed eastern view gives you the dramatic orange "moon illusion."
3. **Go 30 minutes before moonrise.** Your eyes adjust and you catch the rise itself.
4. **Skip the zoom.** A full moon is bright — phones blow it out. Tap to lower exposure.

## What's a "Blue Moon," really?

Two definitions float around: the **monthly blue moon** (the second full moon in a single calendar month) and the **seasonal blue moon** (the third of four in one season). Neither has anything to do with color — though wildfire smoke and ash can genuinely tint the moon blue. When a month has full moons near its 1st and 31st, you get the monthly kind.
